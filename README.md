# EpiMob Transfer Entropy

**EpiMob Transfer Entropy** is a toolkit for uncovering *which human mobility signals actually drive the spread of an epidemic* — and how far in advance they do so. During the COVID-19 pandemic, mobility indicators such as contact rates and short- or mid-range movement often shifted weeks before case counts responded. This project turns that intuition into a rigorous, reproducible measurement by applying **Transfer Entropy**, an information-theoretic measure of directed influence between time series. Instead of asking whether two signals merely correlate, it asks a sharper question: does knowing the past of a mobility signal genuinely help predict the future of infections, beyond what the infection curve already tells us about itself?

At its core, the toolkit ingests province-level time series of mobility and COVID-19 indicators for France, Austria, Spain, and Italy, available at both weekly and daily resolution. For any chosen input feature (e.g. contact rate), output feature (e.g. cases or deaths), and time lag, it estimates the **Effective Transfer Entropy (ETE)** — a shuffle-corrected, statistically validated version of Transfer Entropy that filters out spurious information flow — along with a p-value and a normalized score (NETE) that makes results comparable across zones and features. This lets researchers rank candidate predictors by their genuine, lead-time-aware predictive power, making it a practical tool for **feature selection in epidemic forecasting**.

The project is organized to be easy to run and easy to extend. Ready-to-use datasets live in `Input_Data/`, the core estimation logic sits in a well-documented `Modules/utils.py`, and `Scripts/` provides two entry points: `Experiment.py` runs a worked example on the bundled mobility/COVID data, while `Experiment_input_bivariate_time_series.py` lets you point the same machinery at *any* bivariate time series of your own — just supply a CSV with an index, a zone label, and your `x` and `y` columns. Results are saved as tidy CSV files under `Output_Experiments/`, with all intermediate and final estimates preserved for inspection. The `Notebooks/` folder walks through the whole pipeline interactively, from loading the data to launching an experiment to reading back the results.

Whether you are an epidemiologist hunting for early-warning indicators, a data scientist building forecasting models, or a researcher exploring causal structure in any pair of time series, this project gives you a transparent, well-tested path from raw signals to statistically grounded, directional insight. Clone it, open `Notebooks/launching_experiment_script.ipynb`, and you can reproduce a full Transfer Entropy experiment in minutes — then swap in your own features, countries, or lags to make it your own. For a deeper walkthrough of the data schema, entropy components, and output structure, see [`code_description.md`](code_description.md).
